# Concept Mapping

## Overview
Concept Mapping is a visual tool for organizing and representing knowledge through concepts and their relationships. Unlike mind maps (which radiate from a center), concept maps show directional relationships between concepts with labeled connecting lines.

## When to Use

### Ideal Situations
- **Learning complex topics**: Understanding relationships between concepts
- **Knowledge synthesis**: Integrating information from multiple sources
- **Teaching**: Helping others see conceptual relationships
- **Requirements analysis**: Mapping system components and dependencies
- **Research planning**: Organizing literature and theoretical frameworks
- **Assessment**: Evaluating understanding of a domain
- **Strategic planning**: Showing how initiatives and goals connect

### Not Recommended For
- Simple linear processes (use flowcharts)
- Brainstorming (use mind maps)
- Hierarchical structures only (use org charts)
- Quick note-taking (too time-intensive)

## How to Use

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Identify Focus Question or Topic**
   - Define what you're trying to understand
   - Frame as a question is helpful
   - Example: "How does climate change affect ecosystems?"

2. **List Key Concepts**
   - Brainstorm important concepts related to topic
   - Can be objects, events, processes, or ideas
   - Usually 15-25 concepts for a good map
   - Example: temperature, CO2, ocean, species, habitat

3. **Rank Concepts by Generality**
   - Most general concepts at top
   - Most specific at bottom
   - Identifies hierarchical structure
   - Example: Ecosystem → Climate → Temperature

4. **Begin Constructing Map**
   - Place most general concept(s) at top
   - Add progressively more specific concepts below
   - Can have multiple branches
   - Non-linear—concepts can connect across branches

5. **Connect Concepts with Linking Lines**
   - Draw lines between related concepts
   - Add linking words/phrases on lines
   - Creates propositions (concept - link - concept)
   - Example: "Temperature → increases → Sea level"

6. **Add Cross-Links**
   - Connect concepts across different branches
   - Shows non-hierarchical relationships
   - Often where creative insights emerge
   - Example: Connect "Species migration" to "Economic impact"

7. **Refine and Revise**
   - Rearrange for clarity
   - Add missing concepts
   - Clarify linking phrases
   - Improve visual layout

8. **Review for Completeness**
   - Do propositions make sense?
   - Are relationships accurate?
   - Any missing connections?
   - Is hierarchy clear?

- Tool for learning and assessment

- Concepts organized from general to specific

## Example Use Cases

### Learning: Photosynthesis
**Focus Question**: How does photosynthesis work?

**Concepts**: Photosynthesis, Plants, Light, Chloroplast, CO2, O2, Glucose, Energy, Water

**Sample Propositions**:
- Plants → perform → Photosynthesis
- Photosynthesis → requires → Light
- Photosynthesis → requires → CO2
- Photosynthesis → requires → Water
- Photosynthesis → produces → Glucose
- Photosynthesis → produces → O2
- Photosynthesis → occurs in → Chloroplast
- Glucose → stores → Energy

**Cross-Link**: O2 → is breathed by → Animals (connects to respiration)

### Business: Product Launch
**Focus Question**: What factors determine product launch success?

**Top-Level**: Product Launch Success

**Branches**:
- Product Quality → affects → Customer Satisfaction
- Marketing Strategy → influences → Market Awareness
- Timing → impacts → Competitive Position
- Price Point → determines → Accessibility

**Cross-Links**:
- Market Awareness → affects → Sales Volume
- Customer Satisfaction → leads to → Word of Mouth → increases → Market Awareness

### Research: Literature Synthesis
**Focus Question**: How do factors influence employee retention?

**Concepts**: Employee Retention, Job Satisfaction, Salary, Work-Life Balance, Career Growth, Management Quality, Company Culture

**Map**: Shows complex web of how these factors interrelate

## Tips for Success

### Creating Maps
- **Start broad**: Begin with most general concepts
- **Clear propositions**: Each link should read as valid statement
- **Specific linking phrases**: Avoid vague "is related to"
- **Cross-links**: Don't just make hierarchies; show integration
- **Visual clarity**: Avoid overlapping lines; use colors

### Group Mapping
- **Start individually**: Each person creates draft
- **Share and discuss**: Compare maps
- **Merge collaboratively**: Create consensus map
- **Richer understanding**: Group maps > individual maps

### Using for Learning
- **Before learning**: Map prior knowledge
- **During learning**: Add concepts as you learn
- **After learning**: Create summary map
- **Compare**: Before vs. after shows growth

## Common Pitfalls
- **Unlabeled links**: Lines without phrases aren't meaningful
- **Vague links**: "Is related to" doesn't show how
- **Only hierarchy**: Missing cross-links loses integration
- **Too simple**: Stopping at 5-10 concepts isn't enough depth
- **Too complex**: 50+ concepts becomes unreadable
- **No focus question**: Map wanders without clear purpose
- **Circular reasoning**: Concepts that define each other

## Concept Maps vs. Mind Maps

### Concept Maps
- Multiple entry points and centers
- Relationships are labeled
- Directional (arrows show flow)
- Focus on propositions
- More structured, formal
- Better for showing causation

### Mind Maps
- Single center, radial structure
- Relationships implied, not labeled
- No specific direction
- Focus on association
- More free-form, creative
- Better for brainstorming

## Novak's Concept Mapping in Education

### Origins
- Developed by Joseph Novak in 1970s
- Based on Ausubel's learning theory
- Used for teaching and assessment
- Shows how knowledge is structured

### Educational Applications
- **Pre-assessment**: What do students already know?
- **Formative assessment**: Track learning progress
- **Summative assessment**: Evaluate final understanding
- **Collaborative learning**: Groups build shared maps

## Scoring Concept Maps (For Assessment)

### Criteria
- **Propositions**: 1 point per valid, meaningful proposition
- **Hierarchy**: 5 points per valid level
- **Cross-links**: 10 points per valid, significant cross-link
- **Examples**: 1 point per specific example

### Quality Indicators
- Complexity (number of concepts and links)
- Hierarchy (levels of generality)
- Cross-links (integration)
- Accuracy (correctness of propositions)

## Key Insight
> "The real learning happens in the links, not the concepts."

Anyone can list concepts. Deep understanding is revealed in how you connect them—the nature of relationships, the cross-links that show integration, the hierarchy that shows structure.

## When to Choose Concept Mapping

**Choose Concept Maps When:**
- Understanding complex relationships
- Need to show causation and dependencies
- Learning or teaching conceptual domains
- Synthesizing knowledge from multiple sources
- Want labeled, explicit relationships

**Choose Mind Maps When:**
- Brainstorming or ideation
- Quick personal note-taking
- Radial structure fits naturally
- Don't need explicit relationship labels

**Choose Other Diagrams When:**
- Flowcharts: Linear processes
- Org Charts: Strict hierarchies
- Network Diagrams: Technical systems

## Advanced: Expert vs. Novice Maps

### Novice Maps
- Fewer concepts (10-15)
- Linear, chain-like
- Few cross-links
- Surface-level relationships

### Expert Maps
- Many concepts (25+)
- Branching, integrated
- Multiple cross-links
- Deep, nuanced relationships

Comparing maps reveals level of expertise and understanding.
