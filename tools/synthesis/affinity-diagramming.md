# Affinity Diagramming

## Overview
Affinity Diagramming is a collaborative method for organizing large amounts of qualitative data (ideas, observations, user feedback) into meaningful groups based on their natural relationships. It reveals patterns and insights through bottom-up categorization.

## When to Use

### Ideal Situations
- **User research synthesis**: Organizing interview notes, survey responses, observations
- **Post-brainstorming**: Making sense of many ideas
- **Problem analysis**: Grouping symptoms, causes, or issues
- **Requirements gathering**: Organizing feature requests or needs
- **Retrospectives**: Categorizing lessons learned
- **Journey mapping**: Organizing customer pain points and opportunities
- **Large data sets**: When you have 50+ individual pieces of information

### Not Recommended For
- Small data sets (<20 items) where categories are obvious
- Highly structured data that fits clear pre-defined categories
- When time is extremely limited (takes 1-3 hours for large sets)
- Quantitative data better suited for statistical analysis

## How to Use

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Gather Your Data**
   - Collect all individual pieces of information
   - Each should be on a separate note/card
   - Examples: Post-its, note cards, digital sticky notes
   - Typical: 50-200+ individual items

2. **Write One Idea Per Note**
   - Keep it brief but understandable
   - Use participant's actual words when possible (user research)
   - Make each note atomic (one thought)
   - Example: "Checkout process confuses me"

3. **Spread Out All Notes**
   - Display all notes randomly on wall/table/digital board
   - Everyone should be able to see all notes
   - Don't organize yet—just spread them out

4. **Silent Sorting (Individual Phase)**
   - Each person silently moves notes into groups
   - Group items that feel related
   - Don't overthink—go with intuition
   - No talking during this phase (critical!)
   - Time: 15-30 minutes

5. **Discuss and Refine Groupings**
   - Now discuss the emerging groups
   - "Why did you group these together?"
   - Move notes if group agrees
   - Split large groups, merge small ones
   - Look for natural affinities

6. **Create Group Headers**
   - For each cluster, create a descriptive label
   - Label should capture the essence of that group
   - Use a different color/size for headers
   - Example headers: "Payment Confusion", "Slow Load Times", "Unclear Navigation"

7. **Create Super-Headers (If Needed)**
   - For large data sets, group headers into themes
   - Creates hierarchy: Notes → Groups → Themes
   - Example super-headers: "Usability Issues", "Performance Problems", "Content Gaps"

8. **Review and Validate**
   - Step back and look at complete diagram
   - Does it make sense?
   - Any outliers or notes that don't fit?
   - Any missing connections?

9. **Document and Synthesize**
   - Photograph or save digital version
   - Write summary of key themes
   - Count items per category (shows weight/importance)
   - Extract insights and recommendations

## Expected Outcomes

### What This Method Produces
- Organized categories from unstructured data
- Visual representation of patterns and relationships
- Consensus understanding among team
- Hierarchical structure (items → groups → themes)
- Prioritization based on frequency
- Actionable insights from qualitative data

### Success Metrics
- All items are categorized (or marked as outliers)
- 5-10 major groups identified (if too many/few, may need refinement)
- Group names are clear and descriptive
- Team agrees on categorization
- Clear themes emerge
- Insights lead to decisions or actions

## Tips for Success

### Preparation
- **One idea per note**: Don't combine multiple thoughts
- **Use participants' words**: Especially for user research
- **Sufficient notes**: 50-200 items is ideal range
- **Physical or digital**: Both work; choose based on team location

### Sorting Process
- **Enforce silence**: Critical for unbiased grouping
- **Move quickly**: Don't overthink, trust intuition
- **Allow reorganization**: Groups evolve; that's good
- **No pre-defined categories**: Let structure emerge naturally

### Group Creation
- **Right size groups**: 3-8 items per group typically
- **Descriptive names**: Not too generic, not too specific
- **Outliers okay**: Some items don't fit; that's informative too

### Team Dynamics
- **No hierarchy during sorting**: Everyone equal
- **Build on each other**: Someone moves note, others respond
- **Convergence is goal**: Eventually groups stabilize

## Common Pitfalls
- **Talking during sorting**: Breaks the silent, intuitive process
- **Pre-conceived categories**: Forcing data into expected groups
- **Too many/few groups**: 15+ groups might need super-categories; 2-3 groups might be too broad
- **Vague labels**: "Miscellaneous" or "Other" aren't helpful
- **Solo without iteration**: Affinity works best with multiple perspectives
- **Not documenting**: Forgetting to capture final diagram

## Affinity Diagram vs. Other Methods

### vs. Thematic Analysis
- **Affinity**: Bottom-up, intuitive, collaborative, visual
- **Thematic**: More rigorous, individual, iterative coding

### vs. Card Sorting
- **Affinity**: Open sorting (no predetermined categories)
- **Card Sorting**: Often closed (sort into given categories)

### vs. Clustering (Statistical)
- **Affinity**: Qualitative, human judgment
- **Clustering**: Quantitative, algorithmic

## Combining with Other Methods

### Affinity + Brainstorming
- Use brainstorming to generate ideas
- Use affinity to organize them

### Affinity + Pareto Analysis
- Use affinity to categorize
- Count items per category
- Apply 80/20 rule to prioritize

### Affinity + Journey Mapping
- Collect pain points/needs
- Affinity diagram to organize
- Map to journey stages

## Key Insight
> "Affinity Diagramming doesn't impose structure on your data—it reveals the structure that's already there."

The power is in letting patterns emerge naturally rather than forcing data into pre-conceived boxes. The silent sorting is critical—it allows collective intuition to surface connections that analytical thinking might miss.

## When to Choose Affinity Diagramming

**Choose Affinity When:**
- Large amount of qualitative data
- Need team consensus on categorization
- Structure isn't obvious
- Want to discover unexpected patterns
- Collaborative synthesis needed

**Choose Other Methods When:**
- Data already fits clear categories
- Working solo with small dataset
- Need statistical rigor (use quantitative clustering)
- Time severely limited

## Practice Exercise
Try affinity diagramming your daily tasks:
- Write each task you do on separate notes (aim for 30+)
- Shuffle them randomly
- Group without overthinking
- What categories emerge?
- Do they match how you thought you spent time?
- Any surprises?

This reveals your actual time allocation vs. perceived allocation.
