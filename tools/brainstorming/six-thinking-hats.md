# Six Thinking Hats

## Overview
Six Thinking Hats, developed by Edward de Bono, is a parallel thinking method that uses six metaphorical "hats" to explore different perspectives on a problem. Each hat represents a distinct mode of thinking, allowing systematic exploration without conflict.

## When to Use

### Ideal Situations
- **Team decision-making**: When diverse perspectives are needed
- **Complex problems**: When issues have multiple facets to consider
- **Conflict resolution**: When people are locked in opposing viewpoints
- **Risk assessment**: When you need balanced analysis of opportunities and threats
- **Product/strategy reviews**: When evaluating proposals comprehensively
- **Innovation workshops**: When generating and evaluating new ideas
- **Meeting facilitation**: When you want structured, productive discussions

### Not Recommended For
- Emergency decisions requiring immediate action
- Simple problems with obvious solutions
- When team is unfamiliar with the method (requires brief training)
- One-person decision-making (though can be adapted)

- **Focus**: Risks, problems, weaknesses
- **Ask**: What could go wrong? What are the flaws? Why might this fail?
- **Devil's advocate, but constructive**
- Example: "The budget seems unrealistic. We've underestimated integration complexity before."

### ðŸ’› Yellow Hat: Optimism and Benefits
- **Focus**: Opportunities, advantages, positive outcomes
- **Ask**: What are the benefits? Why would this work? What's the upside?
- **Constructive positive thinking**
- Example: "This could differentiate us from competitors and open new market segments."

### ðŸ’š Green Hat: Creativity and Alternatives
- **Focus**: New ideas, possibilities, lateral thinking
- **Ask**: What are alternative approaches? How else could we solve this?
- **Free thinking, no judgment**
- Example: "What if we partnered with them instead of competing? Or used AI to automate this entirely?"

- **Focus**: Managing the thinking process, big picture
- **Ask**: What hat should we wear now? What have we learned? What's next?
- **Meta-level orchestration**
- Example: "Let's spend 5 minutes in Green Hat mode, then switch to Black Hat to reality-check ideas."

## How to Use

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Define the Problem/Decision**
   - Clearly state what you're thinking about
   - Example: "Should we launch Product X in Q3?"

2. **Establish Blue Hat Context**
   - Facilitator wears Blue Hat
   - Explain the process
   - Set agenda: which hats, in what order, for how long

3. **Use Hats Sequentially (Everyone Wears Same Hat)**
   - Whole team wears one hat at a time
   - Parallel thinkingâ€”no debate, just contributions from that perspective
   - Typical sequence: Blue â†’ White â†’ Green â†’ Yellow â†’ Black â†’ Red â†’ Blue

4. **White Hat: Gather Facts**
   - "What do we know about market conditions? Customer needs? Technical feasibility?"
   - List information, identify gaps

5. **Green Hat: Generate Ideas**
   - "What are all possible approaches? Let's brainstorm without judgment."
   - Encourage wild ideas, quantity over quality

6. **Yellow Hat: Find Benefits**
   - "What's exciting about these ideas? What value do they create?"
   - Build on positive possibilities

7. **Black Hat: Identify Risks**
   - "What could go wrong? What are the weaknesses?"
   - Critical analysis, but constructive

8. **Red Hat: Express Feelings**
   - "How does this feel now? Any gut reactions?"
   - Brief emotional check-in, no justification needed

9. **Blue Hat: Synthesize and Decide**
   - "What did we learn? What's our conclusion? What's next?"
   - Facilitator summarizes and guides toward action

10. **Switch Hats as Needed**
    - Can revisit hats if needed
    - Example: After Black Hat reveals issues, return to Green Hat for solutions

## Expected Outcomes

### What This Method Produces
- Comprehensive exploration of all angles
- Reduced conflict (everyone wears same hat simultaneously)
- Balanced perspective (positive, negative, creative, factual)
- Clearer decision-making process
- Team alignment on reasoning
- Documented thinking from multiple perspectives

### Success Metrics
- All perspectives have been heard
- Reduced defensive arguing
- Risks and benefits both identified
- Creative alternatives generated
- Decision feels well-rounded and defensible
- Team buy-in improved

## Example Use Cases

### Product Launch Decision
- **White**: Market size data, competitive analysis, development costs
- **Green**: Alternative launch strategies, creative marketing approaches
- **Yellow**: Market opportunity, revenue potential, brand building
- **Black**: Timing risks, resource constraints, competitive responses
- **Red**: Team excitement but concern about readiness
- **Blue**: Decision to launch with phased rollout to mitigate timing concerns

### Hiring Decision
- **White**: Candidate's experience, qualifications, references
- **Green**: How role could evolve, alternative team structures
- **Yellow**: What candidate brings, cultural addition, growth potential
- **Black**: Skill gaps, culture fit concerns, onboarding time
- **Red**: Good feeling about passion, slight concern about communication style
- **Blue**: Hire with 90-day focused onboarding plan

### Process Improvement
- **White**: Current process takes 5 days, costs $10K, 15% error rate
- **Green**: Automation options, workflow redesigns, outsourcing possibilities
- **Yellow**: Could reduce time 80%, free up team for higher-value work
- **Black**: Implementation cost $50K, learning curve, integration challenges
- **Red**: Team is excited but worried about job security
- **Blue**: Proceed with pilot, involve team in design, address concerns transparently

## Tips for Success
- **Enforce the hat**: Everyone wears the same hat at the same timeâ€”no mixing
- **Blue Hat facilitates**: Someone needs to manage process and time
- **No debate during hats**: Save discussion for Blue Hat synthesis
- **Time-box each hat**: 5-10 minutes usually sufficient
- **Visual reminders**: Use actual colored paper, slides, or icons
- **Practice makes perfect**: Team gets better with repetition
- **Safe space for Red Hat**: Emotions are valid, no justification needed
- **Black Hat isn't negative**: It's risk management, not pessimism

## Common Pitfalls
- **Mixed hats**: Slipping into other modes (e.g., being critical during Yellow Hat)
- **Skipping hats**: Neglecting Black or Red because they're uncomfortable
- **Too much time**: Dwelling too long on one hat
- **No Blue Hat**: Forgetting to synthesize and decide
- **Fighting the hat**: "I don't feel optimistic" during Yellow Hatâ€”try anyway
- **Solo use confusion**: Method designed for groups; adapting for individuals takes practice

## Key Insight
> "The main difficulty of thinking is confusion. We try to do too much at once. The six hats allow us to conduct our thinking as a conductor conducts an orchestra." â€” Edward de Bono

By separating thinking into distinct modes, we eliminate the confusion of trying to be creative, critical, and factual simultaneously. Parallel thinking is more productive than argumentative thinking.
